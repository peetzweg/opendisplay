Encryption & Export Compliance

Effective June 15, 2026 · applies to the OpenSidecar Mac and iOS apps

OpenSidecar uses no non-exempt encryption. The apps contain no proprietary or non-standard cryptographic algorithms, and they implement no encryption of their own. Any transport security the apps have — or gain in the future — comes from the standard encryption built into macOS and iOS, which is exempt under the relevant export regulations. Both apps therefore declare ITSAppUsesNonExemptEncryption = false in their Info.plist.

What this page is

Apps distributed through the App Store must state whether they use encryption, and U.S. export regulations (and Apple's review process) require documentation for apps that use non-exempt encryption. This page records OpenSidecar's classification and the reasoning behind it, so the claim is auditable rather than asserted. It accompanies Apple's own guidance on complying with encryption export regulations.

What encryption the apps use today

OpenSidecar streams your Mac's screen to your device over a single direct connection. As of this writing:

TransportEncryption
USB The video travels through your cable via the usbmuxd service built into macOS. OpenSidecar adds no encryption of its own; the link is point-to-point over the cable and never touches a network.
WiFi The video currently travels unencrypted across your own local network (see the privacy page for the full caveat).

The apps make no HTTPS calls, contact no servers, and ship no cryptographic library, key material, or proprietary algorithm. There is, today, no encryption of app data to classify.

What changes with encrypted WiFi (issue #16)

Encrypted, authenticated WiFi transport is on the roadmap (issue #16). The planned design uses TLS via Apple's Network.framework (NWProtocolTLS) with a pre-shared key established by a short pairing code. This does not change the export classification:

  • It uses the standard encryption provided by the operating system, not a cryptographic implementation bundled into the app.
  • TLS and TLS-PSK are standard protocols published by international standards bodies (the IETF — RFC 8446 and RFC 4279), not proprietary or non-standard algorithms.
  • The encryption secures the app's own communications between a user's own devices; it is not an encryption product in itself.

Encryption that is limited to using the standard facilities of the operating system is exempt, so the ITSAppUsesNonExemptEncryption = false declaration remains correct after issue #16 ships. This page will be updated if that ever stops being true.

Why the documentation requirement does not apply

Apple requires additional export documentation when an app contains:

  • encryption algorithms that are proprietary or not accepted as standard by international standards bodies (e.g. IEEE, IETF, ITU); or
  • standard encryption algorithms instead of, or in addition to, the encryption used by or accessible within Apple's operating system.

OpenSidecar matches neither case. It implements no proprietary cryptography, and it adds no encryption beyond what the operating system itself provides. The documentation requirement therefore does not apply.

Declaration

Both the Mac and iOS apps set, in their Info.plist:

KeyValue
ITSAppUsesNonExemptEncryptionfalse

This key is configured in the project's project.yml for both targets, so it is part of the public source rather than entered by hand at submission time.

Verifiability

OpenSidecar is open source under the GPL-3.0 license. The complete source code of both apps is public at github.com/peetzweg/opensidecar. You don't have to take our word for any of this — you can read the networking code, confirm there is no bundled cryptography, and watch the connection with the tools of your choice.

This page is provided for transparency and to document OpenSidecar's self-classification. It is not legal advice. Anyone redistributing or modifying the apps is responsible for their own export-compliance determination.

Changes and contact

If the apps' use of encryption changes, this page and the ITSAppUsesNonExemptEncryption declaration will be updated in the same repository — the edit history is public. Questions are welcome on the issue tracker.
